Supportability is the ability of a system to be effectively supported, diagnosed, and improved by internal and external stakeholders.

## Description

Supportability extends operability beyond the core engineering team to include support staff, partners, and users. It ensures that systems can be understood, diagnosed, and maintained by a broader audience. Supportable systems provide clear interfaces, documentation, and diagnostic capabilities. This quality is essential for long-term sustainability and scalability of operations.

## Risks

* Dependence on a small number of experts
* Slow response to user issues
* Difficulty in onboarding new team members
* Poor user experience and satisfaction
* Increased operational costs

## Symptoms

* Frequent escalation of issues to core engineers
* Lack of clear documentation or support processes
* Long resolution times for user-reported issues
* Difficulty reproducing user problems
* Fragmented support tooling

## Causes

* Insufficient documentation and knowledge sharing
* Lack of user-facing diagnostics
* Poor system transparency
* Overly complex or opaque system design
* Absence of support workflows

## Development

* Provide comprehensive documentation and training
* Implement user-facing diagnostics and error reporting
* Standardize support processes and tools
* Design systems with transparency and clarity
* Enable self-service capabilities

## Maturity

A supportable system empowers a wide range of stakeholders to understand and resolve issues. Support teams can diagnose problems without deep system knowledge, and users can often resolve issues independently. Knowledge is well-documented and accessible.

At higher maturity, supportability becomes proactive. Systems provide clear feedback, self-healing mechanisms, and guided diagnostics. Support becomes efficient, scalable, and integrated into the system’s lifecycle.

## Trade-offs

* Time and effort in documentation and tooling
* Potential exposure of internal system details
* Increased complexity in designing user-facing diagnostics
* Resource investment in training and support infrastructure

## Example

A digital twin platform for ecosystem restoration required expert intervention to diagnose issues. Local operators struggled to understand system outputs and errors.

The system introduced clear documentation, user-friendly dashboards, and guided diagnostics. Support teams could resolve issues quickly, and local operators gained confidence in using the system. Supportability enabled broader adoption and long-term sustainability.

## Summary

Supportability ensures that systems can be sustained and evolved over time by a wide range of stakeholders. It complements operability and observability by extending their benefits beyond core engineering teams.
